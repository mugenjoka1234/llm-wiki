# Mini Review: ChatGPT Pulse

### ChatGPT's new daily digest feature is much more than it seems in the story of our relationship with AI.

[![Matthew Becher's avatar](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Kprm!,w_36,h_36,c_fill,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe94e0f82-5248-4bb2-b28b-86f67b77b4cf_493x493.jpeg)](https://substack.com/@matthewbecher)

Since ChatGPT’s public debut, OpenAI has steadily nudged its flagship product from a chatbot into something more proactive. Part of AI’s latent potential, however you may feel about it, is its ability to foresee, not just react, to your needs. The recent release of Pulse in September isa meaningful shift toward that, engaging with us not only when we ask, but when we don’t.

Launched as a **mobile-only preview for ChatGPT Pro users**, Pulse is a daily feed of personalized conversation cards. It pulls from your memory, past chats, trending content, and even your calendar (if you let it) to deliver AI-suggested starting points each morning. Cards can be news, use cases, updates, reminders, and small nudges — maybe ideas for a side project you’ve mentioned, a follow-up, or a fresh angle on your goals.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RuHH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1c166a6a-9df7-4a3a-9ef3-9961c7a00458_1179x2088.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RuHH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1c166a6a-9df7-4a3a-9ef3-9961c7a00458_1179x2088.png)

This kind of proactive AI isn’t new. Tools like **Rewind, Humane, and Rabbit** have also explored it — but Pulse is the first time OpenAI has offered a system-level glimpse into what an embedded agent might feel like inside the ChatGPT ecosystem. Every morning, it pings me with an invitation to the day’s digest, which I’ve been reading most days with my morning coffee. While the tool will eventually be made at lower subscription tiers (and perhaps eventually to all users), I’ve got your preview of how the experience might change your relationship with ChatGPT.

Subscribe

Pulse’s job is to start the conversation before you do. Every day, it asynchronously “thinks” about your saved memory, chat history, feedback, and connected apps. The result is a set of visual, tappable cards that you can click into, give feedback on, and spin out into new conversations with ChatGPT.

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uMtt!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F422d8328-235b-4c95-bd12-7dff11ecf36f_1179x2410.png)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uMtt!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F422d8328-235b-4c95-bd12-7dff11ecf36f_1179x2410.png)

There’s structure underneath: safety filters, ranking algorithms, memory-based personalization, and a kind of agentic “overnight research.” There’s also a daily prompt for you to tell it what you want to hear more of, and some suggested topics. How you interact with the cards (thumbs up/down, bookmarking, starting conversations) also guides its suggestions. So how does that actually feel in practice?

[![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z0dV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F50a8891f-9cf8-4092-b23f-b8f7c2268008_1179x1383.jpeg)](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z0dV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F50a8891f-9cf8-4092-b23f-b8f7c2268008_1179x1383.jpeg)

* * *

Initially, I found each digest to be a buffet of interesting ideas. I was bookmarking, asking questions, and collecting conversations for later reference. Since I ask ChatGPT about a broad range of topics and it was connected to my Google calendar, it had a much better idea of what I was interested in than I expected.

In hindsight, this isn’t a huge surprise. Your search and browsing activity have been constantly monetized to try to sell you things for years. Yet the conversational format meant I got very specific, detailed, and helpful suggestions on everything from personal projects to the best plans with suggested routes for my weekend. It was profoundly helpful and a little unsettling.

Eventually, it started repeating itself and returning to the same conversations, so I had to actively prompt it to share different things and think for myself on what I might want to see more of. There are only so many hours in a day, a lot of the seeds of great ideas I’ve collected haven’t been revisited since they require active attention. I did, however:

- Learn a lot more about the connection between HRV and post-workout recovery.

- Program a simple options trading application in Python after seeing some example workflows.

- Build a guide on ethically setting complex style and lighting settings in Midjourney.

- Set up a new preset into my camera and buy a better lens for candid photography with a Kodak Gold 200 aesthetic.


Which has all been pretty fun! All of these things could’ve been accomplished without Pulse, but the bite-sized daily prompting helped me inch forward on these projects without any of them feeling too overwhelming or preoccupying.

With enough prompting (and in a future where it has more agentic capabilities), this framework could lead to vast improvements in managing day-to-day schedules, tackling projects, deepening niche knowledge, and maybe even managing personal relationships. As is always the case, your mileage varies depending on how much you actively craft the output.

* * *

We’re at the crest of rising interest in AI agents across the consumer and business sectors. 44% of U.S. consumers say they’d use an AI assistant that acts proactively on their behalf. For Gen Z, that number climbs to ~70%. On the business side, McKinsey projects $3–5 trillion in agent-led commerce annually by 2028 ... but adoption is uneven. Only 2% of UK businesses have fully deployed agent systems, despite 93% of executives seeing them as a competitive advantage. Pulse is a proof point: an example of how agents might quietly slide into mainstream tools and change how we interact with them, in closed environments and across multiple apps.

Pulse sits on the threshold of something larger: the shift from prompt-based chat to autonomous assistance. What might that look like? Agents that don’t just suggest, but act by scheduling, purchasing, resolving, and coordinating? Ongoing mandates like “help me get in shape,” “build a weekly recap,” or “run my inbox” executed continuously, not transactionally? What do you delegate, and how do you know the agent got it right? How do you strike a balance between getting help and giving up too much?

When social media feeds shifted behavior from seeking out and curating content to passively receiving it, that shift wasn’t necessarily a good thing. Now that we’re on the edge of having even smarter content fed to us, will it make us smarter, more curious, and more productive, or will passivity make it just another feed to mindlessly scroll through?
